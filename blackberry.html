Blackberry Wine

Jump to Instructions

Blackberry wine is a luscious and flavorful beverage that's perfect for sipping on a cozy evening or sharing with friends and family. With its deep, rich color and sweet-tart taste, blackberry wine is a great way to enjoy the taste of summer all year round. Making blackberry wine at home is surprisingly easy, and with just a few simple ingredients, you can create a delicious and impressive batch. So, if you're looking for a fun and rewarding project that will yield a delicious result, try making your own blackberry wine! I always aim for a ten litre batch but this recipe can be scaled up or down.

Blackberries are a great choice for winemaking, as they're packed with natural sugars and acids that are perfect for fermenting. To get started, all you need is some fresh blackberries, sugar, water, and a few basic winemaking supplies. Blackberry wine can be made in a variety of styles, from dry to sweet, depending on your preference. It also pairs well with a variety of foods, from grilled meats to rich chocolate desserts. Whether you're a seasoned winemaker or a curious beginner, making blackberry wine is a fun and rewarding project that will yield a delicious result. So, grab some fresh blackberries and let's get started!

Blackberries grow in abundance in Ireland, and are best harvested in September when they're fully ripe. Avoid ones not fully ripened as this can lead to a sour taste in the wine. Be careful of the thorns!

Ingredients:

  • 2 kilograms of blackberries (fresh or frozen)
  • 2 kilograms of sugar (destrose works best)
  • Pectic enzyme
  • Campden tablets
  • Acid blend (optional)
  • Wine yeast and nutrient
  • 9 litres of water

Equipment:

  • Fermentation bucket with airlock
  • Two five-litre demijohns with airlocks
  • Syphoning hose
  • Straining bag
  • Stirring paddle

Instructions:

  1. Start by washing all your blackberries if you picked from the wild. Not necessary if using frozen.
  2. Sterilise all your equipment that you're going to use.
  3. Add some boiling water into the fermentation bucket.
  4. Add the blackberries into the straining bag, and then into the bucket. This will minimise the amount of fruit lumps in your wine.
  5. Mash up the blackberries inside the straining bag. No need to pulverise them, just a good mashing.
  6. Toss in a teaspoonful of pectic enzyme, acid blend if using it, and yeast nutrient.
  7. Add in the sugar, and then bring up the water to 11 litres, give everything a stir.
  8. Add in one crushed campten tablet, take your first reading and then wait for 24 hours.
  9. After the 24 hours, throw the yeast in and cover the bucket with an airlock. By the next day you should see bubbles moving through the airlock: this means fermentation is taking place.
  10. Leave this for a week, then if bubbling is slowing down, remove the straining bag of blackberries and discard, then syphon from the bucket into your 2 sterilised demijohns, trying to avoid the sediment at the bottom of the bucket.
  11. Leave in the demijohns for about 6 weeks, you should see bubbling stopping completely and the wine will be clearer.
  12. At this point take your second reading. If it is at 1.0, or the same over a 5 day period, then it's ready to bottle.
  13. Your wine is ready to drink now but will improve with time as the complex flavors intertwine. Can be aged for up to 2 years.
  14. Enjoy!

Tips:

  • If you want to carbonate and make a sparkling wine, add 10 grams of sugar to a 750ml bottle and leave for 2 weeks in a warm location. Make sure to use a pressure resistant bottle to avoid a bomb!
  • Blackberry wine can take a long time to clear. No problem if it's still opaque after 2 months, the taste will still be great.
  • Using frozen blackberries will make the process easier as they release juices easier when frozen.
  • This will produce a deep, sweet wine, perfect as a dessert wine.
  • A bucket with a built-in tap will make the initial racking to demijohns easier.
